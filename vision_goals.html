Strategic Vision and Mission

Vision

By 2036, the Oman Medical Specialty Board will be the **leading digitally integrated authority** for postgraduate medical training, assessment, accreditation, and credentialing in the Gulf region – delivering seamless, **data-driven**, secure, and **user-centric services** that elevate educational quality, operational efficiency, and workforce readiness for Oman’s healthcare system.

In essence, OMSB envisions a future where every interaction – from resident application to specialist certification – is streamlined through intuitive digital platforms, underpinned by real-time data exchange and intelligent automation. This digital vision directly supports OMSB’s core mandate of producing world-class medical specialists and upholds **Oman Vision 2040’s** call for technologically advanced education and health institutions.

Mission

Leverage **secure, scalable cloud-native platforms** and advanced technologies (AI/ML, blockchain, immersive simulation, interoperability standards) to unify OMSB’s processes and data, drive **evidence-based decision-making**, and foster a culture of continuous innovation, collaboration, and service excellence in postgraduate medical education and regulation.

This digital mission aligns with OMSB’s organizational mission to ensure high-quality medical training and certification. It translates that mission into the digital era by ensuring OMSB’s services are efficient, accessible, and interconnected. The strategy commits to bridging current gaps so that OMSB can fully support Oman’s healthcare workforce development and set an example of digital excellence in governance and education. Ultimately, the mission is to transform OMSB’s operating model such that **digital enablement becomes a core strength** – allowing OMSB to serve stakeholders better, make informed decisions quickly, and adapt to future challenges proactively.

Current State Assessment

A candid assessment of OMSB’s current digital state reveals a mix of progress and pressing challenges. While important groundwork has been laid in recent years, several pain points hinder OMSB’s ability to deliver seamless digital services today. The key findings are:

Fragmented Systems & Data Silos

  • OMSB’s digital ecosystem is **fragmented across multiple standalone systems** that do not communicate effectively. Core workflows (e.g. resident registration, exam management, accreditation) are only partially digitized and require manual handoffs.
  • There is **no single “source of truth”** for data – multiple databases (LMS, CPD, HR, accreditation, etc.) operate in silos with inconsistent data definitions.
  • For example, OMSB **lacks a Master Professional Index** to uniquely identify each trainee across all services. Leadership currently lacks unified dashboards for decision-making.
  • External stakeholders (like employers) **cannot verify OMSB-issued certifications in real time**, reflecting the need for integration and data unity.

Limited Service Digitization & User Experience Issues

  • Many OMSB services still involve **paper-based or in-person steps**, leading to inefficiencies and user frustration.
  • As of 2024, **0% of credential equivalency verifications are fully automated**, and roughly **35% of workflows** require physical documents or manual processing.
  • Users must juggle **multiple portals and logins** (for residency, CPD, e-library, exams), as there is no single sign-on or unified interface.
  • **Mobile access is minimal** and real-time notifications (e.g. for exam results or upcoming deadlines) are lacking. This disjointed user experience results in lower satisfaction and adoption of digital channels.

Institutional Readiness Gaps

  • OMSB’s internal digital maturity is uneven. Clinical educators and some trainees are experimenting with new tools (e.g. ~65% of faculty use AR/VR simulation quarterly), but administrative staff have **limited digital proficiency and change-management experience**.
  • Only about **22% of OMSB’s admin staff** have completed formal digital skills training, and **no structured digital literacy program** exists.
  • Governance structures for IT and data are nascent – there is currently **no dedicated IT governance committee or data governance body**, and digital initiatives are managed in silos.
  • Security policies exist on paper but lack enforcement; for instance, **no centralized Security Operations Center (SOC)** exists and incident response tends to be reactive. These gaps pose operational risks and slow transformation efforts.

Compliance and Integration Shortfalls

  • In a recent government digital excellence evaluation, OMSB scored **76/100 overall** – respectable, but below the “excellence” benchmark.
  • OMSB scored very low on certain compliance requirements - for example, **0/10 on adopting the National Digital Accessibility Policy** and similarly low on having an official Internet Use Policy.
  • OMSB’s systems are **not yet integrated with key national platforms** such as the Ministry of Health’s **Nabd Al Shifa EHR** or the unified government services portal.
  • This lack of integration limits data sharing and prevents OMSB services from contributing to the “one government” digital ecosystem.

Drivers for Change

The above challenges underscore an urgent need for holistic digital transformation. OMSB must eliminate silos and modernize its services to handle growing trainee volumes and evolving educational needs more efficiently. Stakeholders – especially new generations of doctors – expect quick, online, mobile-friendly services and seamless digital interactions. Externally, the Omani government (led by the Ministry of Transport, Communications & IT) is pushing all agencies to improve digital service maturity, integrate via common platforms, and contribute to **Oman’s Vision 2040** goals of a “distinguished digital level” of public services. OMSB’s transformation is not just about internal efficiency; it is about aligning with this national digital agenda and excelling in the government’s digital transformation framework. In short, transforming now is **imperative** for OMSB to fulfill its mandate of producing world-class specialists in a modern way, to comply with national directives, and to better serve Oman’s healthcare system.
